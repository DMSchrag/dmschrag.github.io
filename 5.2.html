Mastering Tinkering

Physical MVP

First iteration of the wind tunnel

Process (MVP & Iteration)

We approached this as a tinkering process, focusing on building a minimal viable product (MVP) as fast as possible. Instead of fully planning everything, we started building early and figured things out along the way.

We reused a 350W motor from an old milling machine. This created a constraint, but also gave us a clear starting point. Using existing parts helped us move faster into prototyping.

To make the motor usable, we had to design and build a motor adapter. This was a quick solution, not optimized, but good enough for testing.

At the same time, I created a CAD sketch for the funnel shape. This was then laser cut from wood, which allowed us to quickly produce precise parts.

Another part of the system was the motor mount, which was designed and built separately. This shows a co-design process, where different parts are developed in parallel.

After creating all components, we assembled everything into a first MVP. The focus was not on perfection, but on testing functionality as early as possible.

The process was iterative. We build, test, evaluate and then improve. Instead of aiming for a final solution, we focused on learning from each step.

Scaffolding

Some scaffolding was needed to support the process. This included CAD sketches as a guide, access to tools like the laser cutter, and basic examples of how components could be connected.

At the same time, the scaffolding was kept minimal. The idea was not to follow strict instructions, but to allow exploration and adaptation. The materials and partial designs acted as a starting point rather than a fixed solution.

Conclusion

Overall, the project follows a tinkering-based design method: rapid prototyping, working with constraints, testing early and iterating. The MVP helped us understand what works and what needs to be improved in the next version.
